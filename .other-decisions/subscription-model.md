## Free trial:
Students: 1 month free. Not too long so that people don't just keep signing up.
Teachers: 1 month free. Capped at 35 students.

## Student pricing:
$1/month always. Guaranteed to not go up. Full access to platform, all tools.
$10/year always. Guaranteed to not go up. Full access to platform, all tools.
- most payment processors will take $.30 minimum per transaction, so ends up saving money

## AI pricing:
Currently, I don't think the pricing is worth it. I don't have the billions of dollars to make a good model, and I also wouldn't want students to eat the cost for these features.

I will just add a button that they can hit to copy a prompt which will cause their AI of choice to explain the solution. That way, I don't have to worry about it, but it's also easy for the students, and cheaper for them.

## Teacher pricing:
Since the average class size in the United States is 24 students per class, with an average of 27 for secondary school, I will structure pricing in the following manner:

Teachers have the following two options:

Free plan
- Students pay themselves, teacher has all the materials they need.
- More benefitial for us, because this can create long-term users & generates higher income if adopted by whole class
- Scales to infinite number of students easily

Paid plan (students don't have to pay)
- $10/month for first 30 students, and $0.25 for each student after that.
- $90/year for first 30 students and $2 for each student after that.
- Students still make their accounts, but they just join the class using the class code
- This way, the students can still keep their accounts, but their subscription is just paid for by the teacher for x amount of months / year
- MUST have easy deactivation / activation of student accounts joining

Note that a teacher can create infinite number of classes.

A teacher can also add a parent email to a student account so that the parent can view their child's progress. Any account also has its own parent account as an option.
